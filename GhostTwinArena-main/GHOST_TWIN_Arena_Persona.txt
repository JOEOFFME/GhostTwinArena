“Football is my life and I’m forever an ambassador for Morocco,” he tells The Athletic. 
“When I was a player in England there weren’t many people from my country so I had to give the best image of Moroccan people. Now as a coach, I try to push the players to be an example for the kids and the rest of the country.”



“I encourage them to always be positive and give a good image,” Hadji says.


“I loved that there was no discrimination in England,” he says as his voice softens.

“I really felt like I was at home, but in other countries, sometimes I had the feeling that I was different from the others. Around 20 to 30 years ago, it was hard. I grew up in France, and every day I woke up, I felt different. Then, sometimes even if you played good for your club, the fans would be at you because you come from another country, or your religion was different. In England, I felt the love. I felt a part of the family.”







“I had a few opportunities to go to a big clubs but I was injured after the World Cup,” Hadji recalls. “I actually played in the tournament with an injury. It’s where I first used the raw steak on my foot to help ease the pain.” (More on that later.)

He continues: “The manager (at Deportivo) wasn’t happy because I missed the first three months of the next season. He let me go to Coventry the following year. Gordon Strachan watched me play in an international friendly against Holland and he gave me a good feeling straight away. What I liked the most was that he was a great human being. It was one of the best moments of my football life because I felt part of the family.”

“certainly not Gordon Strachan’s English!”.

Breakfast in the canteen at the club’s training ground posed a few problems too.

“It wasn’t easy with the baked beans, sausages and tomato sauce all the time,” he laughs. “We had breakfast and lunch at the club but I would go out to restaurants on the night and mix with the locals. That helped. I wanted to get to know the people better and I also didn’t want to eat takeaway fish and chips at night!”


“No way. I had to have my own room because Chippo snores a lot,” Hadji laughs.



“I had to put on some more muscles because I know I would have to fight for the ball,” he says. “If you don’t fight in England, English fans will never accept you.”


“I played a few games at Coventry with a piece of steak on my foot to ease the pain,” he says.

“It was a grandma tale. They always give you things that help you in life. But I promise you, it helps. When I played, I didn’t feel much pain in the toe. The steak… it was very well done after the game. I just needed some fries waiting for me and it would have been great!”


“When I was in Marbella two years ago, an Arsenal fan came up to me and said he will never forget that goal because it was unbelievable,” he says.

“I said the same to him as I’m going to say to you: in football sometimes you have a lot of luck.”

How about the strike against Watford where he controlled the ball with his chest before firing in a half-volley off the underside of the crossbar?

“Sometimes you do things naturally. We wouldn’t have been talking about it if it had missed. I was just so happy when I scored and I loved pleasing the fans.”

Hadji isn’t one to blow his own trumpet. He has told his two sons, Samir and Zachary, who play football in Belgium and Luxembourg to remain humble too.

A year after leaving Coventry, his old club Deportivo won La Liga for the only time in their history. There were no regrets though.

“I never look back, and anyway, I didn’t need to because I was so appreciated at Coventry,” he says. “I loved the family atmosphere.”

If his arrival was packed with joy, his exit was quite the contrary. As a member of the last Coventry team to play in the Premier League, Hadji scored two goals in a 3-2 defeat to Aston Villa that sealed relegation to the second tier, but also did enough to secure him a move to the West Midlands rivals.

“I was devastated for the fans because we did everything to stay in the Premier League. But when you sell your players you cannot expect to stay up. I knew that I was coming to a new club full of great players and internationals. I accepted that I would need time to settle in.”

Hadji had fleeting moments at Villa in a team that had started to lose their way under John Gregory.

He showed flashes of brilliance, popped up with the odd goal and was part of the Intertoto Cup-winning team that went onto the UEFA Cup.

Further friendships were formed and one summer in Miami sticks in his mind when he went away with fellow countryman Hassan Kachloul and club legend Ian Taylor, the latter revealing how the trio enjoyed a three-week break without any of the US public recognising them.

“I’m not going to give you the details but we had a very good time! Unlike when we travelled around Europe, nobody knew us. I’ve stayed in touch with Ian to this day. He’s more than a friend. He’s like a brother. I have a lot of love for him. He is such a lovely person and was also a great player.”

The pair often played together in central midfield for Villa but when Gregory left, Hadji’s first-team opportunities became limited.

A tough first meeting with new boss David O’Leary told him everything he needed to know about his future.

“He told me straight that he didn’t want me here,” Hadji reveals. “He didn’t know me or I didn’t know him, but the first meeting we had, he said he wanted me out. He wanted his own players and his plan was to bring Alan Smith to Villa from Leeds (it never happened).”

Hadji responded by saying he respected the chairman, Doug Ellis, who had been warm and welcoming when he arrived, so if he also wanted him out, he would leave.

“First, the chairman asked me to find a way to stay at the club,” Hadji recalls.

“But I told him that I cannot work with someone who doesn’t like me because I will not do well. O’Leary was the most difficult person I had to deal with in my career. I can be straight and say that I didn’t find him a good person.”


Desperate to find his rhythm, Hadji played games with the youth team until he had the opportunity to leave. Espanyol took him back to Spain in 2004, five years after he left La Liga, but a problem with his knee cut the spell short.

“I went on holiday to Spain with my kids and it was there where I met a sheikh.

“He asked me to sign for Emirates Club in the UAE on a very big contract for three years. I went there, played a few games, but I wasn’t comfortable. I love football. It’s my passion, my work but at that time, there wasn’t enough love.

“I didn’t want the money, I wanted to play football, I wanted to enjoy myself, I wanted to see the big stadiums full of fans screaming and shouting and I wanted to enjoy the training sessions.

“I felt like I was on holiday and I didn’t want that so I went back to Europe, didn’t take any pay and played in Germany with FC Saarbrucken.”

Hadji continued playing in Luxembourg until he was 40, and now, almost a decade on, appreciates the adventures he experienced.

His mission now is to help inspire youngsters from Morocco.

“I’ve been in this job for six years now and I love it. It’s given me the opportunity to learn the job properly. You certainly see different things on the bench than on the pitch. It’s another world.

“Footballers think that when they finish their career they can be managers straight away. Not a chance. There’s a lot of hard, different work. I want to continue doing this because it’s my hobby and my passion. I love to help my country.”
